⭐ These processes are exam gold

  • Cellular Respiration — makes ATP for cells
  • Gas Exchange — oxygen in, CO₂ out
  • Osmosis — water across membranes
  • Blood Sugar Feedback — insulin control
  • Natural Selection — traits become common
  • Protein Synthesis — DNA → mRNA → protein

Process 1: Cellular Respiration

Purpose: Make ATP energy

Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP
  • Muscle cells use during exercise
  • Yeast breaks down sugar → dough rises
  • Heart/breathing rates ↑ to get more O₂

Process 2: Gas Exchange in Lungs

  • Air enters lungs → reaches alveoli
  • O₂ concentration higher in alveoli than blood
  • O₂ diffuses into blood
  • CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli
  • CO₂ is exhaled

Key: Gases move by diffusion from high → low concentration

Process 3: Osmosis in Cells

  • Water moves across membrane toward solute
  • Very salty outside → water leaves cell
  • Pure water outside → water enters, cell swells
  • Saline solutions prevent swelling in IVs

Process 4: Blood Sugar Negative Feedback

High blood sugar:

  • Glucose rises → pancreas releases insulin
  • Cells take in glucose → blood glucose ↓
  • Returns to normal ✓

Low blood sugar:

  • Body releases hormone → raises blood sugar
  • Stored sugar released → glucose ↑
  • Returns to normal ✓

Process 5: Natural Selection (4-step)

  • Variation: Population has different traits
  • Selection pressure: Environment favors some traits
  • Survival: Helpful traits → more reproduction
  • Gene frequency: Helpful traits become common

Example: Yaks with EPAS1 gene survived better in low oxygen → gene became more common

Process 6: Protein Synthesis

  • Gene in DNA contains instructions
  • mRNA made from DNA code in nucleus
  • mRNA goes to ribosome
  • Ribosome reads mRNA codons
  • Amino acids joined → Protein formed ✓

Process 7: Photosynthesis

Purpose: Plants make glucose

CO₂ + H₂O + light → glucose + O₂
  • CO₂ enters leaf through stomata
  • Water enters through roots
  • Light energy captured • Glucose made & stored
  • O₂ released

What is Istighfaar?

Istighfaar means asking Allah for forgiveness. The common phrase is Astaghfirullah (I seek forgiveness from Allah). The root word is ghafara — to cover, protect, and forgive.

Conditions of Sincere Repentance
  • Stop the sin immediately
  • Feel regret for doing it
  • Ask Allah for forgiveness sincerely
  • Intend not to return to the sin
  • Fix the harm if the sin hurt another person (return stolen items, apologize, etc.)
Important: Shirk & Forgiveness
⚠️ Shirk (associating partners with Allah) is the greatest sin. Allah does not forgive shirk if a person dies without repenting. But if someone repents from shirk before death, Allah can forgive them.
Balance to Remember
📌 Remember for the Final
  • Everyone sins — the best sinners are those who repent
  • Never lose hope in Allah's mercy
  • Do not use Allah's mercy as an excuse to keep sinning
  • Sincere repentance means actually wanting to change
  • Benefits: forgiveness, peace in heart, closeness to Allah

The Islamic Calendar

Overview

The Islamic calendar is also called the Hijri calendar. It is based on the Hijrah (Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ migration from Makkah to Madeenah). It was organized during the time of Umar ibn al-Khattaab رضي الله عنه.

It is a lunar calendar — following the moon. A new month begins with the sighting of the crescent moon. Months are either 29 or 30 days. The Islamic day begins at sunset, not midnight.

AH & Gregorian Difference

AH = Anno Hegirae = Year of the Hijrah. The Islamic year is about 10–11 days shorter than the Gregorian year, so Islamic months shift through the seasons over time (e.g., Ramadan moves earlier each year).

Q1. What are three lessons Muslims can learn from Prophet Daawood عليه السلام?
Muslims can learn courage, justice, and worship from Prophet Daawood عليه السلام. He showed courage when he defeated Jaaloot with Allah's help. He showed justice when he ruled as a king and judged fairly between people. He also showed devotion to worship by reciting the Zaboor beautifully. His story teaches Muslims to trust Allah and use their abilities for good.
Q2. Explain why Istighfaar is important.
Istighfaar is important because every person makes mistakes and needs Allah's forgiveness. When a Muslim sins, they should stop, feel regret, ask Allah for forgiveness, and try not to repeat it. Istighfaar cleans the heart and brings a person closer to Allah. It also teaches humility — the person admits they need Allah's mercy.
Q3. Why was Umar ibn al-Khattaab رضي الله عنه a great leader?
Umar رضي الله عنه was a great leader because he was just, brave, humble, and responsible. He cared for the poor, widows, and orphans, and did not use leadership for pride. He was called Al-Faarooq because he distinguished truth from falsehood. His caliphate showed that a Muslim ruler must obey Allah and treat people fairly.
Q4. Explain the Islamic calendar.
The Islamic calendar is the Hijri calendar. It begins with the Hijrah of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from Makkah to Madeenah. It is a lunar calendar — following the moon. Each month begins with the sighting of the crescent moon and has 29 or 30 days. The first month is Muharram, Ramadhaan is the 9th month, and Dhul-Hijjah is the month of Hajj.
Q5. Summarize the main steps of Hajj.
Hajj begins with intention and Ihram. Pilgrims perform Tawaf (7 circles around the Ka'bah) and Sa'i (Safa and Marwah). They go to Mina, then stand at Arafah — the most important part. After Arafah they go to Muzdalifah, collect pebbles, then stone the Jamarat. They offer sacrifice, cut hair, perform Tawaf al-Ifadah, and finally Tawaf al-Widaa' (farewell Tawaf) before leaving Makkah.

Chapter Summaries
Chapter 1: The Sound of the Shell
British schoolboys survive a plane crash on an uninhabited tropical island. Ralph and Piggy discover a conch shell and use it to call the other boys. Ralph is elected chief over Jack, creating immediate tension. The boys establish their first rule: whoever holds the conch has the right to speak.
Key Points:
• The conch symbolizes order, authority, and civilization
• Ralph's election creates conflict with Jack's desire for power
• Piggy is immediately mocked for his appearance and asthma
• Boys are optimistic and believe rescue is coming
Chapter 2: Fire on the Mountain
Ralph proposes a signal fire to attract rescuers. Jack volunteers his choir to maintain it. The boys get excited and gather wood, but the fire quickly burns out of control. The boy with the birthmark disappears — likely killed in the fire.
Key Points:
• Fire = both civilization (rescue signal) and danger
• First death foreshadows future violence
• Jack's hunters are already distracted from responsibility
Chapter 3: Huts on the Beach
Ralph and Simon try to build shelters while Jack hunts. Most boys prefer playing to working. Jack becomes obsessed with killing and begins chanting "Kill the pig. Cut her throat." His relationship with authority weakens as hunting obsession grows.
Key Points:
• Division of labor shows civilization vs. survival instinct conflict
• Jack's chant foreshadows ritual violence
• Simon's deep connection to nature first revealed
Chapter 4: Painted Faces and Long Hair
Jack and hunters paint their faces, creating masks that psychologically transform them. They kill a sow and place her head on a stick as a trophy. Piggy is beaten and one lens of his glasses is broken. Savagery escalates rapidly.
Key Points:
• Paint allows boys to hide identity and commit violence without guilt
• The sow's head becomes the "Lord of the Flies"
• Piggy's glasses = intellect; breaking one = partial loss of reason
Chapter 5: Beast from Water
Ralph calls an assembly to restore order. A younger boy reports a "beast" in the water, causing panic. Jack dismisses the beast idea and challenges Ralph's leadership. The assembly fails as fear spreads and the boys divide.
Key Points:
• Fear of the unknown beast destabilizes the group
• Jack openly rebels against Ralph's democratic leadership
• The conch begins to lose its power
Chapter 6: Beast from Air
A dead parachutist falls on the island during a storm. Boys mistake the corpse for the beast. Jack exploits the fear to gain followers by promising protection. The conch has nearly lost all authority.
Key Points:
• Misidentification creates irrational, contagious fear
• Jack gains power by weaponizing fear rather than reason
• Civilization is replaced by superstition
Chapter 7: Shadows and Tall Trees
Ralph, Simon, and others hunt the beast. Simon has a seizure in the forest. The group kills a pig and hunters perform a ritual dance mimicking the pig's death — with increasing violence and ecstasy.
Key Points:
• Simon shows signs of mystical awareness of the truth
• Ritual killing dance replaces civilized behavior
• Jack's control over hunters solidifies completely
Chapter 8: Gift for the Darkness
Jack formally abandons Ralph's group and takes most boys to his own camp. A pig's head is placed on a stick as an offering to the beast. Simon encounters the pig's head ("Lord of the Flies") and has a disturbing vision revealing the beast is within the boys themselves.
Key Points:
• The Lord of the Flies becomes the central symbol of evil
• Simon's vision: evil is not external — it is human nature
• Jack's tribe becomes fully savage with rituals and offerings
Chapter 9: A View to a Die
A storm builds. Simon returns with the truth about the beast, but Jack's tribe is mid-ritual. In the chaos and darkness, the boys mistake Simon for the beast and brutally murder him. His body is washed out to sea.
Key Points:
• Simon's murder = the point of no return into complete savagery
• The mob acts on fear and ritual with no logic

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1 — Box Plot, Five-Number Summary & Outliers

Data (already ordered): 4, 8, 8, 11, 14, 15, 21, 22, 24, 60

Median (Q2) = (14 + 15) / 2 = 14.5
Lower half {4, 8, 8, 11, 14} → Q1 = 8
Upper half {15, 21, 22, 24, 60} → Q3 = 22
IQR = Q3 − Q1 = 22 − 8 = 14
High boundary = Q3 + 1.5·IQR = 22 + 21 = 43
60 > 43 → 60 is an outlier ✓
Five-number summary: 4, 8, 14.5, 22, 60
Tip: A value is an outlier only if it falls below Q1 − 1.5·IQR or above Q3 + 1.5·IQR — always show the boundary calculation.
Example 2 — Line of Best Fit & Interpretation

A calculator's linear regression on (hours studied, test score) gives:

ŷ = 8.2x + 45,   r = 0.94

Slope 8.2 → each extra hour studied predicts
about an 8.2-point higher score.
y-intercept 45 → predicted score with 0 hours.
r = 0.94 → strong positive correlation.

Predict the score for 5 hours:
ŷ = 8.2(5) + 45 = 41 + 45 = 86
Interpolation vs extrapolation: Predicting at x = 5 is interpolation (inside the data) and is reliable; predicting at x = 40 would be extrapolation and far less trustworthy.

✍️ Short Answer Practice

Try answering, then reveal.

Q1. Explain why organisms at the TOP of a food chain are most affected by pollutants that build up in tissues.
Through bioaccumulation, each consumer eats many prey, concentrating the toxin. Top predators eat the most contaminated prey and accumulate the highest pollutant levels.
Q2. A plant is placed in total darkness for 24 hours. What happens to its photosynthesis rate? Explain.
Photosynthesis stops or greatly decreases because light is a required reactant — the reaction cannot proceed without it.
Q3. Explain how insulin maintains blood sugar homeostasis.
When blood sugar rises, the pancreas releases insulin. Insulin signals cells to absorb glucose, lowering blood sugar back to normal. This is negative feedback — the response reverses the original change.
Q4. Explain how a mutation could be BENEFICIAL to an organism.
If a mutation produces a protein that helps an organism survive better in its environment (such as resistance to a disease), that mutation is beneficial. Organisms with the mutation survive and reproduce more, passing it to offspring.
Q5. Why does removing a species from an ecosystem often have unexpected effects?
Species are interconnected in food webs. Removing one species causes its prey to overpopulate and its predators to lose a food source, disrupting the balance of the entire ecosystem (cascade effect).

The old belief that Earth is the center of the universe, with everything revolving around it. Supported by the Church; disproved by Copernicus & Galileo.
Scientific Method
A process for testing ideas using observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and conclusion. The foundation of modern science.
Enlightenment
18th-century intellectual movement applying reason to human society, government, and rights. Directly inspired by the Scientific Revolution.
Natural Rights
Rights people are born with that cannot be taken away — life, liberty, and property (Locke). Central to both the French and Latin American Revolutions.
Peninsulares
Spaniards born in Spain who held the highest rank in the colonial class system of Latin America.
Creoles
People of Spanish descent born in the Americas. Despite being wealthy, they were below Peninsulares and often led independence movements.
Coup d'État
A sudden, often military seizure of power from a government. Napoleon used a coup d'état in 1799 to take control of France.
Napoleonic Code
Napoleon's legal reforms that standardized French law. Spread across Europe through conquest.
Reign of Terror
A radical phase of the French Revolution (1793–94) led by Robespierre. Thousands were executed by guillotine as enemies of the revolution.
Nationalism
Pride in one's nation and desire for self-governance. Fueled by both the French Revolution and Latin American independence movements.
Self-Determination
The Enlightenment idea that people have the right to govern themselves rather than be ruled by a foreign power — key idea for Bolívar.
